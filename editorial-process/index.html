Peer review

Transparent from deposit to decision.

JORN’s proposed workflow combines independent editorial judgment with a public, versioned record of the research and—where appropriate—the review itself.

The proposed workflow¶

01

Deposit

The author deposits a licensed, versioned manuscript in an appropriate open repository. Data, software, protocols, and other objects are deposited in services suited to preserving them.

02

Connect

The submission identifies every relevant research object alongside ORCID records, CRediT roles, ethics, funding, prior versions, and competing interests.

03

Editorial screening

An editor assesses scope, plausibility, integrity, repository eligibility, licensing, ethics, evidence availability, and the need for specialist review.

04

Independent review

Qualified reviewers evaluate scientific validity, methods, reporting, reproducibility, data, and software. Research articles normally receive at least two reviews.

05

Revision

Authors deposit a new version and respond to reviews. JORN records what changed, further comments, and the editor’s assessment.

06

Acceptance & certification

JORN identifies the exact accepted version, publishes the decision and review record, registers publication metadata, and links the complete research record.

07

Continuing stewardship

The journal maintains corrections, expressions of concern, retractions, metadata updates, appeals, and the integrity of its certification.

Open peer review¶

JORN is working toward a review model that makes the reasoning behind certification visible while protecting participants where openness could introduce genuine risk. The founding board will define when reviews are signed, anonymous, public during evaluation, or published after acceptance.

Whatever model applies, authors and reviewers will know the terms before participating. Conflicts will be documented and editorial decisions will be made by an unconflicted editor.

What reviewers assess¶

  • Scientific and methodological validity
  • Statistical analysis and reporting
  • Reproducibility and documentation
  • Data availability and provenance
  • Software quality, testing, and licensing
  • Ethics and responsible research practice
  • Alignment with protocols or preregistration
  • Clarity, accessibility, and contribution

Editorial independence¶

Sponsors, infrastructure providers, partner communities, and the supporting organization will not determine editorial outcomes. Submissions from editors or board members will be handled independently under a published conflict-of-interest procedure.

The journal will publish routes for appeals and complaints before formal submissions open.
